The Dolomite Advantage
Super White occupies a unique middle ground. Harder and more acid-resistant than calcite marble (Calacatta, Carrara) but softer and more etch-prone than true quartzite (Taj Mahal, White Macaubas). This positions it as the practical compromise — you get the luminous white marble aesthetic with meaningfully better performance. But it is not bulletproof. Clients wanting zero-maintenance should choose quartzite or engineered quartz. Clients wanting marble beauty with improved durability choose Super White.
05 DESIGN PRINCIPLES

FIRST PRINCIPLES

The why behind the rules

Principle 01 — Geological Drama Over Classical Refinement
Unlike Italian marbles that speak of cultural refinement, Super White speaks of geological force. The breccia pattern reads as tectonic event — fractured stone reassembled by pressure and time. This gives it a rawer, more elemental character. Design with this energy: Super White suits spaces that want to feel powerful rather than precious. It reads as natural architecture, not decorative luxury.
Principle 02 — The Identity Clarity Imperative
Super White is the most mislabeled stone in the industry. Sold as quartzite, granite, and calcite marble by different suppliers, its true identity as dolomitic marble determines everything: maintenance protocol, acid sensitivity, pricing expectations, and longevity. Understanding what you are actually specifying is not academic — it prevents client disappointment, contractor confusion, and maintenance failures. Always verify classification before committing.
Principle 03 — Cool Dominant, Warm Supporting
Super White is fundamentally cool-toned — white ground, gray veining, occasional blue hints. Successful palettes add warmth through supporting elements: natural wood tones, brass or gold hardware, warm white paint (not bright white), and lighting at 2700K. Without deliberate warming, Super White spaces risk feeling clinical. The material provides the cool architecture; everything else brings the warmth.
Principle 04 — Embrace Imperfection
Dolomitic marble develops character over time — subtle etching, micro-patina, the softening of polished surfaces into lived-in warmth. This is a feature, not a defect. Clients who view natural aging as damage should choose engineered alternatives. Clients who understand that a kitchen counter tells the story of meals prepared, wine spilled, and life lived — they are the right client for this stone. Set this expectation at project start.
Principle 05 — The Breccia Pattern Demands Scale
Super White's fractured-island pattern contains both large quiet areas and dense vein networks. Small cuts lose the pattern's drama — you get fragments of fragments. This stone performs best on large uninterrupted surfaces: full-slab islands, continuous backsplashes, floor-to-ceiling feature walls. Give it room to tell its geological story. Mosaic or small-format cuts waste this material's primary asset.

The Dolomite Maintenance Reality
Super White's dolomite composition gives it a genuine maintenance advantage over calcite marble — acids react more slowly, the surface is harder, and the stone is denser. But “better than marble” does not mean maintenance-free. Clients sold this stone as quartzite will expect quartzite performance and be disappointed. Set expectations honestly: this is a durable marble, not an indestructible surface. The maintenance commitment is real but manageable with proper habits.
13 PITFALLS

CRITICAL WARNINGS

What not to do

Never Trust the Trade Name
Super White is the most mislabeled stone in the industry. Sold as quartzite (Mohs 7), granite, and calcite marble by different suppliers, its true identity as dolomitic marble (Mohs 3.5–4) determines everything about care, durability, and expectations. Always verify classification through independent testing or trusted suppliers. The wrong label creates the wrong expectations.
Never Promise Quartzite Performance
If a client was told this is quartzite, they expect acid resistance, scratch resistance, and zero maintenance. Dolomitic marble delivers none of these at quartzite levels. The disappointment gap between quartzite expectations and dolomite reality is where lawsuits and bad reviews live. Correct the classification before installation, not after.
Never Skip Sealing
At 0.18% water absorption and 0.53% apparent porosity, Super White needs professional sealing before installation and maintenance sealing every 6–12 months. Skipping this step results in staining, moisture damage, and client regret that no amount of cleaning will resolve.
Never Use Acidic Cleaners
Vinegar, lemon-based cleaners, and bathroom products with acid will etch dolomite. The reaction is slower than with calcite marble but it is real and cumulative. pH-neutral stone cleaner only. Read every product label. “Natural” cleaners are often the worst offenders.
Never Install Outdoors
Acid rain and freeze-thaw cycles degrade dolomitic marble over time. Despite being harder than calcite marble, Super White is not suitable for uncovered exterior applications. UV exposure and thermal cycling cause surface degradation and structural stress.
Never Buy Sight Unseen
Super White has extreme slab-to-slab variation in breccia pattern density, vein color intensity, and background tone. Some slabs lean blue-gray, others pure white. Photos cannot capture the depth and character accurately. Always view actual slabs in person, especially for book-matching or large installations.
Never Ignore Fissure Ratings
Lab testing shows fissure resistance of 0.32 and cracking resistance of 0.37 — lower than companion Pegasus dolomites. This means hairline fissures are possible and care during fabrication and installation is essential. Verify slab integrity before cutting. Support large overhangs adequately.
Never Cut Small Format
Super White's breccia pattern contains large-scale geological drama that requires area to read properly. Cutting into small tiles, mosaic, or narrow strips destroys the pattern's narrative. This stone was meant for full slabs, large islands, and expansive surfaces. Small cuts waste the material's primary design asset.
