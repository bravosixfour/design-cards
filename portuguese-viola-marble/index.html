05 DESIGN PRINCIPLES

THE WHY BEHIND THE RULES

Six principles for Viola

01 / Echo Secondary, Not Primary
Viola’s primary statement is the warm beige-white ground. Its secondary elements are the purple-gray veining and dark charcoal deposits. When selecting pairings, echo the purple and charcoal in small accents — blackened steel hardware, plum velvet cushions, smoky gray glass. Never compete with the warm ground by adding more beige; let the marble own that frequency.
02 / Temperature Equilibrium
Viola runs warm (beige ground) with cool undertones (purple-gray veining). This dual temperature is its superpower — it bridges warm and cool palettes naturally. Lean into whichever direction the room needs: brass and oak amplify warmth; brushed nickel and walnut emphasize the cool veining. Either direction works because the stone already contains both temperatures.
03 / One Star Per Stage
When Viola is the star material, everything else in the room must defer. Cabinetry becomes supporting cast — quiet painted finishes or simple shaker profiles. Wall treatments recede to soft neutrals. Lighting fixtures serve the stone rather than competing with it. Only one element commands attention per sightline; Viola is dramatic enough to fill that role completely.
04 / 30% Maximum Coverage
Never let Viola exceed 30% of visible surfaces in a room. A bathroom vanity (10%) plus a feature wall accent (15%) creates ideal impact at 25%. A kitchen island (15%) plus backsplash (5%) works at 20%. Beyond 30%, the polychromatic drama overwhelms rather than elevates — the room becomes about the stone rather than the life lived within it.
05 / Material Honesty
Portuguese Viola is not Italian Calacatta. It has its own identity — warmer, more organic, more approachable. Do not deploy it as a substitute for Italian marble; deploy it as what it is. The Alentejo origin, the undefined veining, the purple warmth — these are features, not compromises. Honest deployment respects both the material and the space it serves.
06 / The Polychromatic Advantage
Most marbles operate on a two-color system (white ground plus gray veining). Viola operates on four or more: beige-white ground, purple undertones, dark charcoal veining, and occasional warm spots. This polychromatic nature means it pairs with a wider range of materials than typical marble — it contains enough colors within itself to bridge diverse palettes.

13 PITFALLS

COMMON MISTAKES

What not to do

01 / Confusing with Italian Calacatta Viola
Consequence: Client expectations misaligned with material reality and pricing.
Prevention: Always specify “Portuguese Viola” or “Estremoz Viola.” The Italian version has a brighter white ground, bolder veining, and costs 2–4× more. Material honesty prevents disappointment.
02 / Skipping Slab Selection
Consequence: Significant color and pattern mismatch across installed surfaces.
Prevention: Always view actual slabs before purchase. Viola has significant slab-to-slab variation. Request detailed photography. For large installations, select consecutive slabs from the same block for book-matching.
03 / Underestimating Maintenance Commitment
Consequence: Etching, staining, and premature aging that frustrates clients.
Prevention: Have an honest conversation about marble maintenance before specifying. Kitchen applications require 3–6 month sealing cycles. If client cannot commit, recommend engineered quartz alternative.
04 / Exterior Installation
Consequence: Freeze-thaw damage, UV color fading, accelerated weathering.
Prevention: Never specify Viola for uncovered exterior applications. The purple pigments are UV-sensitive. Marble is inherently unsuitable for freeze-thaw environments. Use granite or quartzite for exterior.
05 / Competing Materials in Same Sightline
Consequence: Visual chaos; Viola’s polychromatic drama overwhelmed or undercut.
Prevention: Apply one-star-per-stage rule. If Viola is the star, everything else defers. Never pair with another dramatic stone. Never mix marble types on the same surface. Keep supporting materials quiet.
06 / Exceeding 30% Surface Coverage
Consequence: Space feels dominated by stone rather than enhanced by it.
Prevention: Calculate Viola’s percentage of visible surfaces. Island (15%) + backsplash (5%) = 20% is ideal. Above 30%, the polychromatic pattern creates visual fatigue rather than delight.
07 / Cool Lighting Selection
Consequence: Viola’s warm ground appears washed out; purple tones read gray; polychromatic effect flattened.
Prevention: Specify 2700K–3000K lighting exclusively in rooms with Viola. Warm light activates the full color spectrum. Cool light (4000K+) kills the stone’s warmth entirely.
08 / Ignoring Data Quality Limitations
Consequence: Specifying based on estimated rather than certified performance data.
Prevention: Contact LSI Stone directly for certified ASTM lab reports on specific slab lots. Technical data in this card is derived from regional marble standards, not Viola-specific testing. Always verify before critical structural applications.
