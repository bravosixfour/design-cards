01 DEFINITION
TERM CARD

Cornice

A projecting horizontal molding assembly at the junction of wall and ceiling, creating visual termination through shadow and profile. The architectural capstone that resolves the wall’s upward journey.

Etymology
From Italian cornice (frame, ledge), from Latin corona (crown, wreath) — the crowning element
Function
Creates visual termination at wall-ceiling junction through projection and shadow
Defining Test
Does it project outward from the wall plane at ceiling height to cast a shadow line?
Cornice IS
A projecting horizontal element at wall-ceiling junction — may be single molding or built-up assembly of multiple profiles creating depth, shadow, and visual termination for the wall plane
Cornice is NOT
Crown molding alone (one component type) • Entablature (complete classical assembly: architrave + frieze + cornice) • Cove lighting tray • Exterior roofline cornice (structural) • Ceiling medallion

Taxonomy/ the cornice family

CORNICE
├── BY CONSTRUCTION
├── Simple — single molding profile (crown, cove, bed)
├── Built-up — multiple moldings layered for depth
└── Integral — cast as single piece (plaster, polyurethane)

├── Cove — concave curve, soft shadow

├── Dentil — tooth-like blocks, Greek origin
└── Flat stock — rectangular, modern minimal

├── Wood — pine, poplar, hardwoods

├── Plaster — traditional, ornate
└── Polyurethane — lightweight, moisture-resistant

├── Classical — Greek/Roman orders, dentil, egg-and-dart
├── Georgian/Federal — refined profiles, moderate scale
├── Victorian — elaborate, heavy profiles
├── Craftsman — simple, flat or subtle cove
└── Modern — minimal or absent
03 TYPES

FROM SIMPLE TO COMPOUND

Types/ the cornice spectrum

Cornice ranges from a single strip of cove molding to elaborate built-up assemblies with dentil blocks, egg-and-dart enrichment, and multiple stepped profiles. The type you choose establishes the room’s formality ceiling — everything else must live beneath it.

SIMPLE CROWN
Single profiled molding — cove, ogee, or combination — installed at the wall-ceiling junction. The most common residential solution. Profile complexity varies but construction remains single-piece. Standard stock sizes from home centers.
Profile: Single piece, 3.5″–5.25″ (89–133mm)
Typical projection: 2–4″ (51–102mm)
Cost indicator: $–$$
BUILT-UP CORNICE
Multiple molding pieces layered to create depth and visual complexity. Crown atop bed mold atop flat frieze. Classical method for achieving scale beyond single-piece stock limitations. Requires careful layout and compound mitering skill.
Profile: 2–5 components stacked
Typical projection: 5–12″ (127–305mm)
Cost indicator: $$–$$$
COVE CORNICE
Concave profile creating a gentle curve from wall to ceiling. Softer shadow than angular profiles. Traditional in bedrooms, transitional spaces, and Craftsman interiors. Reads as understated elegance rather than classical authority.
Profile: Simple concave arc
Typical projection: 2–6″ (51–152mm)
Cost indicator: $–$$
DENTIL CORNICE
Incorporates a row of small rectangular blocks (dentils) beneath the crown profile. Greek origin — the “teeth” represent beam ends in trabeated construction. Maximum classical formality. Demands precise spacing calculation.
Profile: Crown + dentil band + bed mold
Typical projection: 6–10″ (152–254mm)
Cost indicator: $$$–$$$$
PLASTER CORNICE
Cast or run-in-place plaster creating seamless ornate profiles. Victorian and Edwardian standard. Allows for enrichment (egg-and-dart, acanthus, rosettes) impossible in milled wood. Requires specialized installation and repair.
Profile: Integral cast, unlimited complexity
Typical projection: 6–18″ (152–457mm)
Cost indicator: $$$–$$$$$
MODERN MINIMAL
Flat stock or subtle L-profile creating a reveal rather than a molded transition. Shadow gap between wall and ceiling replaces profiled projection. Reads as contemporary architecture, not applied decoration. Requires precision drywall work.
Profile: Flat stock or shadow gap
Typical projection: 1–2″ (25–51mm) or reveal
Cost indicator: $$–$$$
04 PROFILES

THE VOCABULARY OF SHAPES

Profile anatomy/ & components

Every cornice is composed from a limited vocabulary of profile shapes. These shapes have names — ogee, cove, ovolo, cavetto, cyma recta, cyma reversa — and each creates a distinct shadow character. Understanding the vocabulary enables intelligent specification and period-accurate design.

Crown Molding
The primary angled profile bridging wall and ceiling. Springs from the wall at 38° or 45° (common stock angles). Face may incorporate ogee, cove, or combination curves. The workhorse component — often the only piece in simple installations.
Stock sizes: 3.5″–6.25″ · $1–$8/LF
Bed Molding
Smaller angled profile installed beneath the crown in built-up assemblies. Adds a secondary shadow line and increases total projection. Classical bed molds feature cove or ogee faces. Transition element between crown and flat surfaces below.
Stock sizes: 1.5″–2.5″ · $0.60–$3/LF
Dentil Molding
Strip of evenly-spaced rectangular blocks representing classical beam ends. Installed horizontally in built-up cornice assemblies. Dentil size and spacing must relate to overall cornice scale — oversized dentils on small cornice reads as clumsy.
Stock sizes: 3⁄4″–1.5″ · $2–$12/LF
Flat Frieze
Horizontal flat board installed below the crown, creating a vertical band. Expands cornice depth without additional profiled molding. Can remain plain or receive applied ornament. Essential for achieving classical proportions in built-up work.
Stock sizes: 3″–8″ wide · $0.50–$2/LF
Cove Molding
Concave quarter-round profile. Used as transition piece between flat surfaces or as the primary cornice in simple installations. Creates a soft, curving shadow. Common in Craftsman and Mid-Century contexts where angular profiles feel too formal.
Stock sizes: 3⁄4″–4.5″ · $0.40–$4/LF
Enrichment
Applied or integral decorative elements — egg-and-dart, bead-and-reel, acanthus leaf, Greek key. Transforms plain profiles into period-specific ornament. Wood carved, plaster cast, or polyurethane molded. Requires matching to profile scale and style era.
Varies by type · $5–$50+/LF

PROFILE GEOMETRY

Ogee (Cyma Recta)
S-curve with convex portion at top, concave at bottom. The most common crown profile. Creates strong shadow line with classical character.
Cyma Reversa
Inverted ogee — concave at top, convex at bottom. Less common, often used in bed molds and transitional pieces. Softer shadow than cyma recta.
Ovolo
Quarter-round convex profile. Simple, bold shadow. Often used in combination with other shapes. Greek origin — the “egg” in egg-and-dart molding.
Cavetto
Quarter-round concave profile. The inward curve of cove molding. Egyptian origin — represents palm frond at column capital. Soft, receding shadow.
05 PRINCIPLES

THE WHY BEHIND THE RULES

Design principles/ the logic of cornice

01
Scale follows room height
Cornice projection must be proportional to ceiling height. Classical rule: cornice depth equals 1/12th of room height. An 8′ (2.4m) room takes a 4″ (102mm) cornice; a 10′ (3m) room takes a 5″ (127mm) cornice. Undersized cornice disappears; oversized cornice oppresses.
02
Cornice is the room’s crown
Just as a crown signifies the head’s importance, cornice signals the ceiling’s terminus. It must be the most assertive horizontal molding in the room. If baseboard or chair rail dominates visually, the hierarchy inverts and the room feels top-heavy or unresolved.
03
Shadow is the design
The cornice profile is merely the mechanism; the shadow it casts is the actual design element. A complex profile in flat light disappears. A simple cove in raking light creates drama. Always evaluate cornice by the shadow it produces, not the profile shape alone.
04
Cornice speaks the room’s language
Profile complexity must match existing trim vocabulary. If door casing is simple round-over, cornice should be simple cove or ogee. If baseboard features dentil or fluting, cornice can carry equivalent enrichment. Mismatched vocabulary creates architectural dissonance.
05
Built-up creates authority
Multiple stacked components produce visual weight that single-piece cannot achieve. Each layer adds shadow, depth, and perceived cost. Built-up cornice signals intentionality — someone designed this rather than grabbed stock. The effort communicates care.

08 SENSORY

THE FULL EXPERIENCE

Sensory qualities/ how cornice is perceived

Visual
Shadow line is the primary visual element — horizontal band of darkness where profile meets wall. Profile complexity affects shadow character: ogee creates crisp, angular shadow; cove creates soft, gradual gradient. Lighting angle dramatically affects perception — overhead light flattens; side light reveals. White cornice on white wall reads as pure shadow; contrasting colors add explicit boundary.
Spatial
Cornice visually lowers the ceiling by creating a defined edge. Deep projection (6″+) brings the ceiling plane forward. Simple crown maintains perceived height while completing the junction. Built-up cornice with frieze creates the strongest ceiling-drop effect. In high-ceiling rooms, this grounding is desirable; in low-ceiling rooms, it compresses.
Tactile
Rarely touched directly due to height. When encountered — during installation, painting, or cleaning — painted wood and MDF feel smooth and slightly cool. Plaster has more mass and thermal density. Polyurethane feels lightweight and slightly hollow. The weight difference between real plaster and foam replica is immediately apparent in hand.
Acoustic
Cornice subtly affects room acoustics. Profiled surfaces break up sound reflections at the ceiling-wall corner. Deep cove profiles redirect sound. Effect is minor but perceptible in hard-floored rooms with high ceilings. Ornate plaster cornice adds more acoustic complexity than simple crown.

PERCEPTION BY LIGHTING CONDITION

Direct overhead light
Minimizes shadow, flattens profile. Cornice appears as line rather than form. Choose deeper projection if room has primarily overhead lighting.
Side/window light
Maximizes shadow, reveals profile fully. Morning and evening light creates dramatic raking shadows. The ideal condition for appreciating cornice.
Uplighting
Illuminates the underside of cornice, creates reverse shadow. Cove cornice with concealed LED becomes architectural lighting element.
Evening artificial
Table and floor lamps cast upward light, revealing cornice differently than daytime. Shadow intensity varies with lamp position throughout room.
09 SELECTION

DECISION FRAMEWORK

Selection logic/ how to choose

Q1
What is the ceiling height?
8′ (2.4m) → Simple crown 3.5–4.5″, avoid built-up. 9′–10′ (2.7–3m) → Crown 4.5–5.25″ or simple built-up. 11′+ (3.3m+) → Built-up cornice 6–10″ projection appropriate. Ceiling height is the first filter.
Q2
What is the existing trim language?
Study door casing, baseboard, and window trim. Classical profiles (ogee, bead) → crown with matching complexity. Simple profiles (round-over, flat) → simple cove or understated crown. No existing trim → opportunity to establish vocabulary. Match the language.
Q3
What is the design style?
Traditional / Classical → crown with dentil or built-up. Transitional → simple crown or cove. Modern → shadow gap or minimal flat stock. Craftsman → simple cove only. The style dictates the complexity ceiling.
Q4
What is the installer skill level?
DIY basic → polyurethane crown (lightweight, flexible). DIY with miter saw → wood or MDF crown (requires cope or compound miter). Professional → built-up assemblies, plaster, dentil work. Match complexity to capability.
Q5
What is the budget?
DIY simple crown $150–$400 per room. Professional simple crown $400–$1,200. Built-up cornice professional $1,500–$4,000+. Ornate plaster $3,000–$15,000+. Budget determines whether to simplify or hire.
Q6
Paint plan?
Monochromatic (wall color on cornice) → most contemporary, forgiving of imperfect joints. Contrasting (white cornice on color wall) → traditional, reveals every flaw. Ceiling color matching cornice → visually lowers ceiling. Decide before layout.

11 COST

INVESTMENT STRUCTURE

Cost architecture/ budgeting cornice

BUDGET TIER
$2–$5 per linear foot installed
MDF or finger-joint pine crown, 3.5–4.5″. DIY installation or basic contractor. Paint-grade finish included. Standard stock profiles from home centers. Suitable for rental properties, secondary rooms, budget-conscious projects.
MID TIER
$6–$12 per linear foot installed
Poplar or select pine crown, 4.5–5.5″, or simple built-up (crown + bed mold). Professional installation with proper coping or compound miters. Quality paint finish. Suitable for primary rooms in most homes.
PREMIUM TIER
$15–$30 per linear foot installed
Built-up cornice with 3+ components, dentil or enrichment, or large-scale crown (6″+). Skilled trim carpenter required. Multiple coats professional paint. Suitable for formal rooms, historic restoration.
LUXURY TIER
$40–$100+ per linear foot installed
Run-in-place or cast plaster, ornate enrichment, custom profiles. Master craftsman required. Restoration-grade materials and techniques. Suitable for landmark homes, institutional spaces, period-accurate restoration.

HIDDEN COST FACTORS

Outside corners
Each outside corner adds 15–30 minutes labor. Complex profiles require return pieces. Budget $15–$50 per outside corner.
Inside corners
Coped inside corners require skilled labor. DIY often uses corner blocks ($5–$15 each) to avoid coping.
Waste factor
Budget 15–20% extra material for cuts, mistakes, and miscuts. Complex rooms with many corners need more.
Ceiling prep
Wavy or textured ceilings require shimming or scribing. Add 20–40% labor for imperfect surfaces.
12 INSTALLATION

METHODS & CARE

Installation/ & maintenance

METHOD
DESCRIPTION
SKILL LEVEL
TOOLS REQUIRED
Coped Inside Corners
First piece runs square to wall; second piece profile-cut to fit against first. The professional standard — allows for expansion and creates tight joint.
Intermediate–Advanced
Miter saw, coping saw, files
Compound Miter
Both inside and outside corners cut with compound angles (bevel + miter). Requires precise saw setup. Joints may open with seasonal movement.
Intermediate
Compound miter saw
Corner Blocks
Pre-made blocks at corners; straight cuts in between. Eliminates coping and compound miters. Reads as more ornate or traditional.
Beginner
Miter saw (90° cuts only)
Built-Up Assembly
Multiple components installed sequentially — frieze first, then bed mold, then crown. Each layer must be level before next. Labor-intensive but achieves large scale.
Advanced
Miter saw, level, air nailer
Plaster Run-In-Place
Profile template dragged through wet plaster applied to wall-ceiling junction. Traditional method for ornate profiles. Requires skilled plasterer.
Master craftsman
Plaster tools, custom template

MAINTENANCE SCHEDULE

Monthly
Dust with microfiber duster or vacuum with brush attachment. Prevents buildup that yellows paint.
Annually
Inspect for cracks at corners (seasonal movement), nail pops, or caulk separation. Touch up as needed.
Every 5–10 years
Repaint if yellowing or visible wear. Prep includes light sanding and caulk refresh at joints.
As needed
Fill cracks with paintable caulk. Re-nail any loose sections. Sand and repaint damaged areas.
13 PITFALLS

WHAT TO AVOID

Pitfalls/ & warnings

Scale mismatch
Installing 6″ built-up cornice in an 8′ ceiling room. The cornice dominates, the room feels compressed, and the investment reads as misguided rather than elegant. Always proportion cornice to ceiling height.
Vocabulary conflict
Ornate dentil cornice with simple flat-stock baseboard and door casing. The cornice speaks a different architectural language than the room. Profile complexity must coordinate across all trim elements.
Visible caulk lines
Using thick beads of caulk to fill poorly-cut joints. Caulk shrinks, cracks, and yellows. Tight joints with thin caulk disappear; thick caulk advertises the flaw it was meant to hide.
Inadequate blocking
Nailing crown into ceiling drywall alone. Over time, the crown sags or pulls away. Large crown needs backing blocks or nailer strips secured to framing.
Spring angle confusion
Cutting crown at wrong compound miter settings because the spring angle (38° vs 45°) was misidentified. Always verify the spring angle before cutting. Different profiles have different springs.
Foam crown in visible locations
Cheap foam cornice that dents, crushes, and shows seams obviously. Fine for a quick rental upgrade; inappropriate for rooms where quality matters. Know the material limitations.
Skipping primer
Painting MDF cornice without proper primer. The end-grain absorbs paint unevenly, joints telegraph, and the finish looks blotchy. Primer is not optional for MDF.
Moisture exposure
Installing wood or MDF cornice above a shower or in consistently humid environments. The material swells, warps, paint peels. Use polyurethane or PVC in moisture-prone areas, or skip cornice entirely.

15 / TERMINOLOGY CLARITY +
cornice///terminology clarity// cornice vs crown molding/ crown molding is a specific type of angled profile/ cornice is the broader concept of any projecting element at wall-ceiling junction/ a cornice may consist of crown molding alone, or may include crown as one component of a built-up assembly/ all crown molding installations are cornices, but not all cornices are simple crown molding// cornice vs entablature/ entablature is the complete classical assembly above columns: architrave + frieze + cornice/ cornice is only the top projecting portion/ interior “cornice” typically refers to the crown area only/ entablature is exterior or monumental vocabulary// cornice vs cove lighting/ cove lighting tray is a functional element that conceals light source and bounces light off ceiling/ cornice is decorative element creating shadow and visual termination/ cove lighting trays may incorporate cornice-like profiles but serve different primary purpose// interior vs exterior cornice/ interior cornice is decorative molding at wall-ceiling junction/ exterior cornice is structural and water-shedding projection at roofline/ different construction, different materials, different scale/ this TERM card addresses interior cornice only// spring angle terminology/ spring angle is the angle at which crown sits against wall and ceiling/ 38° crown springs 38° from wall (52° from ceiling)/ 45° crown springs equally from both surfaces/ critical for calculating compound miter cuts/ not to be confused with miter angle or bevel angle//
16 / PITFALLS & WARNINGS +
cornice///pitfalls & warnings// skipping primer/ painting MDF cornice without proper primer/ the end-grain absorbs paint unevenly, joints telegraph, finish looks blotchy/ primer is not optional for MDF — use dedicated MDF primer or shellac-based sealer// moisture exposure/ installing wood or MDF cornice above shower or in consistently humid environments/ material swells, warps, paint peels/ use polyurethane or PVC in moisture-prone areas, or skip cornice entirely// ignoring out-of-square conditions/ assuming walls meet ceiling at perfect 90° angles/ few rooms are truly square/ scribe or shim to accommodate real-world conditions/ forcing square cuts into out-of-square corners creates visible gaps// insufficient acclimation/ installing wood cornice immediately from cold warehouse delivery/ wood expands and contracts with temperature and humidity/ let material acclimate in the room for 48–72 hours before installation// over-nailing/ driving nails too close together or too hard/ creates splits in wood and MDF/ pre-drill near ends/ space nails 12–16″ apart/ set nail heads slightly, don’t sink deep// mixing materials in same run/ using MDF for some sections and wood for others due to availability/ different expansion rates cause joints to open/ different paint absorption creates visible texture difference/ commit to one material per room// undersized corner blocks/ using corner blocks that don’t fully accommodate the crown profile/ crown doesn’t meet block cleanly/ always test fit corner blocks with your specific profile before committing//
17 / THE FIRST PRINCIPLE +
cornice///the first principle// why cornice exists/ the wall and ceiling meet at an angle/ this junction is structurally necessary but visually abrupt/ the raw corner reveals the limits of construction — imperfect joints, tape seams, paint lap marks/ cornice transforms this utilitarian junction into an intentional design element/ it converts a problem (the corner) into an opportunity (the crown)// the deeper truth/ cornice exists because humans find abrupt transitions unsatisfying/ we prefer resolution over interruption/ a wall that simply stops at the ceiling feels incomplete/ the cornice provides termination — a visual period at the end of the wall’s sentence/ this is why “cornice” comes from “corona” (crown) — it dignifies what it tops// the transferable insight/ junctions require mediation/ wherever two planes meet, the designer must decide: reveal the joint honestly, conceal it entirely, or celebrate it with intentional detail/ cornice is the celebration strategy/ this principle applies to all design — furniture, graphics, product — not just architecture/ the quality of transitions defines the quality of design// the test of understanding/ if you understand cornice, you can look at any room and know immediately whether the wall-ceiling junction was resolved with intention or ignored by default/ you can specify cornice appropriate to ceiling height, trim vocabulary, and design era/ you can explain why built-up cornice feels more authoritative than simple crown/ you recognize that the shadow, not the molding, is the design//

primary purpose// interior vs exterior cornice/ interior cornice is decorative molding at wall-ceiling junction/ exterior cornice is structural and water-shedding projection at roofline/ different angle//cornice///pitfalls & warnings// skipping primer/ painting MDF cornice without proper primer/ the end-grain absorbs paint unevenly, joints telegraph, finish looks blotchy/ primer is not optional
