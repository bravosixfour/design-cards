01 DEFINITION TERM CARD

Timber
Flooring

Flooring constructed from real wood — either solid planks milled from single timber pieces or engineered boards with genuine wood veneer over layered substrates. The defining characteristic is authentic wood at the surface.

Also Known As
Hardwood Flooring
Wood Flooring / Parquet
US: "hardwood" dominant. UK/AU: "timber" preferred. "Parquet" = patterned installation.
Category
Flooring Material
Natural / Renewable
Biophilic design element. Carbon-sequestering when sourced responsibly.
Primary Types
Solid + Engineered
Two Construction Methods
Solid = one piece of wood. Engineered = veneer over plywood/HDF core.
The Defining Test
Real Wood Surface
Not Image / Not Vinyl
If you cannot sand and refinish the surface, it is not timber flooring.
What Timber Flooring Is NOT
Laminate flooring is a photographic image of wood printed on paper, laminated to HDF. Cannot be refinished. NOT timber.

LVP (Luxury Vinyl Plank) is plastic with printed/embossed wood image. Waterproof. NOT timber.

Wood-look tile is ceramic or porcelain with wood-pattern glazing. NOT timber.

The distinction matters: timber patinas, gains character, can be restored. Imitations degrade and must be replaced.

The Aging Principle
Timber flooring is not a static material. It responds to light, humidity, and use over time. This is a feature, not a flaw. The floor you install today will look different in five years — often more beautiful, with depth and character that no new floor can replicate. Design with this evolution in mind.

The Modern Reality
Engineered hardwood now represents 60%+ of the hardwood flooring market. This is not a compromise — it's evolution. Modern construction uses concrete slabs, radiant heating, and multi-level installations that solid cannot accommodate. Premium engineered floors with thick veneers (4-6mm) are indistinguishable from solid underfoot and offer superior performance in most conditions.
When Solid Is Non-Negotiable
Choose solid hardwood when: (1) matching existing solid floors in renovation, (2) above-grade installation with stable humidity, (3) client values 100+ year lifespan and multi-generation refinishing, (4) historic restoration requiring period-authentic materials. Otherwise, quality engineered is objectively superior for contemporary construction.

The Janka Hardness Scale — Reference Guide
Janka measures the force (lbf) required to embed a 0.444" steel ball halfway into wood. Higher numbers = better dent resistance.

Soft (under 1000): Pine, Cherry, Walnut, Cork — visible wear expected, character from use
Moderate (1000-1300): Red Oak, Beech, Birch — residential suitable, some wear with heavy use
Hard (1300-1800): White Oak, Maple, Ash, Hickory — recommended for pets/traffic
Very Hard (1800-2500): Jatoba, Wenge, Spotted Gum, Mesquite — excellent durability
Extremely Hard (2500+): Ipe, Cumaru, Strand Bamboo — commercial grade, exceptional durability

Note: Janka is one factor among many. Species with lower Janka may be preferred for aesthetic reasons, historic authenticity, or when character from wear is desired.

Grade Is Aesthetic, Not Quality
Lower grades are not inferior wood. The same tree produces Select and Rustic boards — grading sorts by appearance, not durability. Rustic-grade oak is as hard as Select-grade oak. Some designers specifically request character grades for their organic authenticity. Grade selection is purely aesthetic and price-driven.

CUT TYPES

Cut Method Grain Pattern Stability Cost & Availability
Plain Sawn (Flat Sawn) Cathedral / flame patterns, tangential grain Most movement with humidity changes Most common (70%+ of production), most affordable
Quarter Sawn Straight, linear grain with ray fleck figure Significantly more stable than plain sawn Limited availability, 20-30% cost premium
Rift Sawn Very straight grain, minimal figure Maximum stability Rare, 30-50% cost premium, often special order
Rift & Quarter Mix Combination of linear patterns Very good stability Available option, moderate premium
Live Sawn / Through & Through All patterns present — mixed grain orientation Variable (depends on board position) Maximizes log use, often marketed as "character"
Why Cut Matters
Quarter and rift sawing waste more of the log to achieve grain orientation. This waste translates to cost premium but delivers real benefits: better dimensional stability (less seasonal gaps), more linear appearance (contemporary aesthetic), and in oak specifically, reveals ray fleck figure (the shimmering lines prized in Arts & Crafts and Mid-Century design). For radiant heat installations, quarter/rift sawn is strongly recommended.
07 PRINCIPLES

THE WHY BEHIND THE RULES

First principles of timber flooring

Principle 01: The Biophilic Foundation
Timber flooring exists because humans have measurable psychological and physiological responses to natural materials. Studies show wood interiors reduce cortisol levels, lower blood pressure, and increase feelings of wellbeing. This is not aesthetic preference — it's evolutionary biology. Timber earns design space through its effect on human occupants.
Principle 02: The Ground Plane Anchor
Flooring is the largest horizontal surface in any room. It anchors every other design decision. Timber flooring provides warmth that balances cool materials (concrete, steel, glass). It provides organic texture that counters manufactured uniformity. It provides tonal foundation that connects disparate furniture. The floor is not backdrop — it's foundation.
Principle 03: The Continuity Principle
Running the same timber flooring throughout a space creates visual continuity that makes spaces feel larger and more cohesive. Changing flooring at every room boundary fragments perception. Continuity is power. Break flooring only at genuine threshold moments: wet to dry, public to private, dramatic material contrast (wood to stone). Never at arbitrary room boundaries.
Principle 04: The Living Material Commitment
Timber changes. It responds to light (color shifts). It responds to humidity (expansion/contraction). It responds to use (patina, wear patterns). Clients seeking static perfection should choose engineered quartz tile. Timber is for clients who understand that aging is not damage — it's character accumulation. Design with evolution in mind.
Principle 05: The Width-Space Relationship
Plank width affects perceived room scale. Narrow planks (2-3") read traditional and can make small rooms feel busy. Wide planks (7"+) read contemporary and luxurious but can overwhelm small spaces. Match plank scale to room scale. General rule: rooms under 200 sq ft benefit from planks under 5". Large open plans can support 7-12" wide planks.

10 SENSORY

THE FULL EXPERIENCE

Beyond visual/ the complete material

Sense Experience Design Implication
Visual — Color Natural warmth from blonde through chocolate. Every plank unique. Changes with light throughout day. Creates dynamic, living backdrop that responds to natural light cycles.
Visual — Texture Grain patterns provide visual micro-texture even on smooth finishes. Adds interest without pattern; balances solid-color furnishings.
Tactile — Underfoot Warmer than tile or stone. Natural compliance (slight give). Textured finishes add grip. Comfortable for bare feet; reduces fatigue vs. hard surfaces.
Tactile — Temperature Wood is thermal insulator — feels near room temperature rather than cold. Eliminates "cold floor" shock common with tile. Works with radiant heat.
Acoustic Warmer sound than hard surfaces. Some resonance. Can amplify footstep sound. Add rugs in conversation areas; underlayment selection affects sound transmission.
Olfactory Some species (cedar, teak) have natural scent. Most: subtle wood smell when new. New floor smell fades. Oil finishes may have longer scent presence.
The Tactile Warmth Advantage
Timber's thermal behavior is underappreciated. Wood is a natural insulator with low thermal conductivity. Unlike tile (which conducts heat away from bare feet, feeling cold) or concrete (thermal mass that takes room temperature but feels cold), wood stays near ambient temperature. This makes timber ideal for bedrooms, nurseries, and any space where occupants go barefoot. It's not perception — it's physics.
The Aging Patina
All timber flooring develops patina — a combination of color change (UV exposure), micro-wear patterns (foot traffic), and subtle surface evolution. Patina is not damage; it's accumulated character. A well-maintained 20-year-old timber floor often looks more beautiful than when installed. This aging potential is timber's unique value proposition versus static materials.

The Refinishing Advantage
Timber flooring's defining advantage over all alternatives: it can be restored. When polyurethane wears through, you sand and refinish — floor looks new again. When LVP wears through, you replace. 14 WARNINGS

WHAT NOT TO DO

Common mistakes/ terminology traps

Never Confuse Laminate with Hardwood
Laminate flooring is a photographic image of wood printed on paper and laminated to HDF. It is not hardwood. It cannot be refinished. Calling laminate "hardwood" or "wood flooring" is factually incorrect and misleads clients. Use correct terminology: laminate, hardwood (solid), engineered hardwood.
Never Skip Moisture Testing
Installing over concrete without moisture testing is professional malpractice. Concrete can emit moisture for years after pour. Moisture wicking through slab causes cupping, buckling, adhesive failure, mold. Test with calcium chloride or in-situ probe. Acceptable levels: <3 lbs per 1,000 sq ft per 24 hours.
Never Install Without Acclimation
Wood must equilibrate with room humidity before installation. Skip acclimation = immediate expansion/contraction issues. Stack planks in install room for 3–7 days with HVAC running at normal conditions. Engineered is more forgiving but still benefits from acclimation.
Never Skip Expansion Gaps
Wood expands and contracts with humidity changes. Floors installed tight to walls will buckle when humidity rises. Leave ½"–¾" expansion gap at all walls and fixed objects. Cover with baseboard. Floating installations are especially sensitive — follow manufacturer specs exactly.
Never Use Solid Below Grade
Solid hardwood in basements is guaranteed failure. Basement concrete wicks moisture regardless of moisture barrier. Solid wood cupping, mold, and failure are inevitable. Basements require engineered hardwood with proper moisture mitigation — and even then, test and monitor.
Never Wet Mop Hardwood
Standing water damages hardwood finishes and can penetrate seams. Use damp mop (wrung until barely wet), never wet mop. Steam mops are controversial — many manufacturers void warranties with steam use. Excess moisture causes cupping, finish clouding, seam damage.
Never Assume "Engineered" Means Cheap
Engineered hardwood ranges from cheap ($3/sq ft, thin veneer) to ultra-premium ($20+/sq ft, thick veneer, European oak). Quality engineered with 4mm+ veneer outperforms cheap solid in every metric. Don't dismiss engineered — evaluate thickness, core quality, veneer quality, finish.
Never Ignore Janka for Pet Households
Large dogs destroy soft floors. Their nails exert concentrated pressure that dents low-Janka species instantly. For homes with dogs over 50 lbs, specify Janka 1300+ (hickory, white oak). Consider textured/distressed finishes that hide scratches. Or accept that floor will show wear.
